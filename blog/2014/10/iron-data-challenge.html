--- layout: blog root: ../../.. author: Greg Wilson title: "The Iron Data Challenge" date: 2014-10-27 time: "11:30:00" category: ["Tooling"] --- http://www.nsf.gov/statistics/seind02/pdf_v2.htm has links to Excel spreadsheets with data on higher education in science & engineering in the US from the 1970s to the turn of the century; on that page, http://www.nsf.gov/statistics/seind02/append/c2/at02-16.xls has "Earned bachelor's degrees, by field and sex: 1975-98 (selected years)". http://www.nsf.gov/statistics/seind12/appendix.htm; on that page, http://www.nsf.gov/statistics/seind12/append/c2/at02-18.xls has earned bachelor's degrees by sex and field: 2000-2009. The goal is to produce a plot showing how the numbers and gender balance have changed in the physical sciences, the earth/planetary/climate sciences, biological & agricultural sciences, mathematics, computer science, psychology, and the other social sciences over that period. This will be tricky because (a) the spreadsheets are formatted differently, (b) one of them presents a combined total for biological & agricultural while the other doesn't, and (c) there's a lot of other stuff in this spreadsheet that needs to be ignored. So, how quickly can people get from URLs to final plots? And on top of that, what can they uncover in the data about correlations or inverse correlations between different fields? The first is a fixed target, the second is "make the most impressive dessert you can".
